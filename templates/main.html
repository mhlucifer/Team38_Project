Cane Toad Timeline

History

1900s

  • Sugar cane commercially grown in Queensland since the 1860s.
  • Losses caused by white grub attacks became a serious problem in the 1880s.
  • Queensland government established the Queensland Bureau of Sugar Experiment Stations (BSES) in 1900 to address the issue.
  • Entomologists studied cane beetles and investigated solutions for around 25 years, including various control measures.

1930s

  • Government entomologist Reginald Mungomery imported cane toads from Hawaii, believing they could control beetle pests.
  • In June 1935, Mungomery brought 102 toads to Australia and released them in Queensland
  • Despite initial concerns raised by entomologist Walter Froggatt, toad releases continued due to pressure from BSES and cane growers

1930s

  • Froggatt warned about the potential pest status of cane toads but was ignored.
  • No pre-release testing was conducted to assess the toads' impact on native Australian species.
  • BSES and government officials overlooked the risks, and bans on further releases were lifted.

1960s

  • Cane toads rapidly multiplied and spread across Queensland, Northern Territory, New South Wales, and Western Australia.
  • Cane Toads had colonized most of Queensland's east coast.
  • They reached the Northern Territory/Western Australian border by 2009, and by 2023, they were sighted as far south as Bankstown, Sydney.

2000s

  • Despite efforts to control their spread, cane toads remain a significant pest in Australia.
  • Lessons learned from the introduction of cane toads have influenced modern quarantine laws and risk assessment procedures.

Daytime Preference
Cane Toad Behavior

• Cane toads are nocturnal animals. This means that they are most active during the night because of the cooler temperature and the higher moisture levels
• Being nocturnal also helps the cane toads avoid predators and reduces their risk of dehydration in hot and dry conditions during the day
Mating Behaviour
Cane Toad Behavior

• Cane toads exhibit various behaviours to attract its mates for breeding
• Male cane toads produce unique mating calls to attract the females. These calls vary in frequency, duration and intensity. These calls depend on the individual’s size, health and environmental conditions
• Female cane toads are attracted to males with loud and intense calls indicating their health and size
Mobility
Cane Toad Behavior

• Cane toads do not have the athletic ability such as frogs to jump long distances.
• Because of this, cane toads typically stay close to the ground and bound only short distances.
• This limited mobility is a plays a huge part in the cane toads choosing a suitable habitat to live in
